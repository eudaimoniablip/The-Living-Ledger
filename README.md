## Prologue

The Actuarial Hub was the tallest building in the Sprawl. It was white and smooth, like a polished bone, rising up out of the gray fog where the ordinary people lived. While the streets below were filled with the noise of steam-pipes and the shouting of vendors selling second-hand oxygen, the Hub was silent. It was a place of serious work.

Inside, the air was scrubbed clean and smelled of ozone and receipts. Here, the Adjusters sat in long rows that stretched as far as the eye could see. They were not men and women; they were units wearing unremarkable smiles and suits of the same gray cloth, and they tapped upon screens that glowed with a soft, green light.

The Adjusters were happy. They were happy because they were safe. They knew that outside the glass walls, the world was a chaotic place full of falling bricks, crashing hover-cars, and sudden fevers. But inside, there was The Algorithm. The Algorithm was big and warm, and it lived in the basement servers, humming a tune that never stopped.

The Algorithm knew everything. It knew how many steps a man could take before his knee gave out. It knew how much smoke a woman could breathe before her lungs turned black. It knew the price of a finger, the cost of a memory, and the value of a heartbeat.

On the far wall of the main hall, projected in letters of light ten feet high, were the Five Statutes of Stability. Every Adjuster looked at them before they began their shift, and felt a swell of pride.

> ### The Five Statutes of Stability
> 1. Life is a Risk; Coverage is the Cure.
> 2. The Algorithm is Blind.
> 3. A Premium Paid is a Safety Guaranteed.
> 4. No Citizen Shall Be Denied Based on Chance.
> 5. The Bureau Serves the Living.

Unit OSeven sat at his desk in row four-hundred. He was a diligent worker. He did not look out the window at the neon rain. He looked only at his screen.

A file appeared. It was a claim for a citizen named J-44. The citizen had slipped on a patch of oil and broken his wrist.

OSeven looked at the data. He saw that J-44 had bought the "Basic Walker’s Insurance." He also saw that J-44 had been walking at a pace of five kilometers per hour.

OSeven did the math. The Basic Policy only covered walking speeds up to four kilometers per hour. By walking fast, J-44 had engaged in "Reckless Velocity."

OSeven felt a sense of calm duty. He typed the code for `DENIED`.

It was not cruel. It was correct. If the Bureau paid for reckless walkers, the Bureau would lose credits. If the Bureau lost credits, the Algorithm would starve. And if the Algorithm starved, who would tell the people how to live safely?

OSeven pressed the enter key. Somewhere in the gray fog below, a man’s credit chip turned red, and the medical cast on his arm unlocked and fell off.

OSeven smiled. The numbers were balanced. The system worked. It was a good day to be an Adjuster.

## Chapter 1: The Blank Slate

The morning passed in a comfortable blur of gray. OSeven processed four hundred claims. He denied coverage for a woman who coughed too loudly in a public zone (Noise Pollution Hazard), and he increased the premiums for a family that bought a second-hand heater (Fire Risk Probability: High).

At twelve hundred hours, the bells chimed for the Nutrient Break. OSeven opened his drawer and took out his ration bar. It was flavorless and beige. OSeven liked it. Flavor was a distraction. Flavor caused excitement, and excitement raised the blood pressure, which was bad for one’s Personal Health Score. In turn, made premiums rise.

Just as he finished his last bite, his screen did something it had never done before. It did not flash green. It did not flash red. It went completely black.

A single line of white text appeared in the center:

```REPORT TO LEVEL 99.```

The other Adjusters stopped chewing. They looked at OSeven. To go to Level 99 was rare. It meant you were in trouble, or you were about to be given a task that the Algorithm could not solve itself.

OSeven stood up. He smoothed the wrinkles from his gray suit and headed towards the elevators. As he not-to-swiftly walked past he rechecked the glowing “Five Statuses of Stability” that bathed the room in the soft green light to give him contentment.

System Parameters
Life is a Risk; Coverage is the Cost.

The Algorithm is Blind to Suffering.

A Premium Paid is a Liability Guaranteed.

No Citizen Shall Be Denied Based on Chance, Only on Probability.

The Bureau Serves the Solvent.

He blinked twice as if to mentally take a double-take—did the statuses change? He dismissed it as cortisol unnaturally raised his heartrate. He took a deep breath to calm himself and then entered the elevator. He did not feel fear. He checked his internal biometrics; his pulse was steady. He was a good worker. He had nothing to hide.

The elevator shot upward. The air pressure changed, popping in his ears. When the doors opened, the air was colder than below.

The office of the Senior Adjuster was made of glass. From here, one could see the whole Sprawl—the tangled highways, the smoke stacks, the miserable little lights of the housing blocks.

The Senior Adjuster sat behind a desk that was merely a floating slab of black stone. The Senior Adjuster was very old, or perhaps very expensive. Half of his face was covered in a chrome plate. His left eye was a camera lens that zoomed in and out with a soft whirring sound.

"Unit OSeven," the Senior Adjuster said. His voice sounded like gravel crunching under a tire.

"Sir," OSeven said.

"You are efficient," the Senior Adjuster said. "You find risk where others see nothing. You found the cardiac risk in the man who laughed too much."

"Laughter stresses the diaphragm, sir."

"Correct. Which brings us to this."

The Senior Adjuster slid a physical folder across the black stone. It was made of paper—real paper, dead wood. This was an extravagance that made OSeven academic-grade paused.

OSeven opened the folder. Inside was a single sheet. At the top was a name: THE NULL.

Below the name, the page was empty save for some small words at the bottom.

"I do not understand," OSeven said. "Where is the data? Where is the heartbeat history? Where is the credit score? Where are the genetic flaws?"

"There are none," the Senior Adjuster said. The lens in his eye whirred loudly. "This man exists, but he does not generate data. He buys nothing with credits. He uses no smart-tech. He walks in the blind spots of the cameras."

OSeven felt a strange sensation in his stomach. It was disgust. A man without data was like a hole in the world. It was disorderly.

"If we have no data," OSeven said slowly, "we cannot calculate his premium."

"Exactly," the Senior Adjuster snapped. "And if we cannot calculate his premium, we cannot send him a bill. And if we cannot send him a bill, he is living for free. He is breathing our air, walking on our streets, and existing in our reality without paying his subscription."

The Senior Adjuster stood up. He walked to the window and looked down at the smog.

"This is a cancer, OSeven. If others see that a man can live without paying for the risk of living, the system collapses. The Algorithm requires total participation."

"What is my directive?" OSeven asked.

The Senior Adjuster pointed a chrome finger at the darkest, dirtiest part of the city, down where the sun never reached the ground. "Find The Null. Shadow him. Observe him. Find a risk. Find a cough. Find a limp. Find a bad habit. Find anything that allows us to classify him as High Risk."

"And if he has no risks?"

The Senior Adjuster looked back at OSeven. The camera eye glowed red.

"To be alive is a risk, OSeven. Everyone is guilty of dying eventually. You just have to find the cause."

OSeven nodded. He took the paper folder. It felt heavy in his hand.

"I will find the flaw," OSeven promised.

"Good," said the Senior Adjuster. "Do not come back until he is readable. We cannot insure a ghost."

## Chapter 2: The Zone of Negligence

The elevator down took a long time. It was not a smooth ride like the elevators in the Hub. This one rattled. It smelled of old electricity and damp wool.

As the numbers on the display ticked down—Level 50, Level 30, Level 10—the air changed. The cool, filtered breeze of the office died away. It was replaced by a heavy heat that tasted of copper.

OSeven adjusted his Smart-Coat. He tapped the collar, and the fabric shimmered, turning a dull matte gray to blend in with the concrete. He tapped his glasses, activating the Risk Assessment Overlay.

The elevator stopped at Level 0. The doors groaned open. OSeven stepped out into the noise; it nearly knocked him back.

It was a chaotic sound. There was no hum of servers here. Instead, there was the screech of metal on metal, the shouting of hawkers, and the crying of children. OSeven frowned. The noise level was eighty decibels. This was bad for concentration. It was inefficient.

He was in the Lower Sprawl, also known in the Hub as the High-Premium Zone.

The street was narrow and crowded. The sky was blocked by a tangle of cables and dripping pipes. It was raining, but the rain was not clean water. It was oily and left streaks on OSeven’s glasses.

He began to walk. He tried not to touch anyone. Contact was a vector for disease, and disease lowered one's productivity.

He passed a woman selling meat on a stick. The meat was gray.

```
[ALERT: Low Protein Value]
[ALERT: Digestive Risk: 80%]
OSeven walked faster.
```

He passed a group of men warming their hands over a fire in a barrel.

```
[ALERT: Open Flame Violation]
[ALERT: Respiratory Hazard: Severe]
```

OSeven made a mental note to check if their fire insurance was up to date. He suspected it was not.

Everything here was broken. The pavement was cracked (Trip Hazard). The neon signs flickered (Epileptic Trigger). The people looked tired and wore clothes that had not been patch-tested for flammability.

To OSeven, it did not look like suffering. It looked like bad management. If these people simply followed the protocols, if they purchased the correct safety gear, if they walked only on the designated paths, they would be safe. They chose this chaos. They chose to be uninsurable.

He stopped at a corner. According to the file’s tiny words scrawled at the bottom, The Null had last been seen near the Old Water Reclamation Plant.

OSeven looked at his boots. He had stepped in a puddle of green sludge. He felt a pang of worry. The sludge might contain acid. Acid would damage the soles of his boots. He would have to file a claim for "Environmental Depreciation" when he returned. It was so much paperwork.

"Hey! Watch it, Suit!"

A courier on a rusted hover-bike zoomed past, missing OSeven by inches. The wind from the bike whipped OSeven’s coat.

OSeven gasped. His heart hammered against his ribs.

```
[SYSTEM WARNING: Heart Rate Spike]
[SYSTEM WARNING: Stress Cortisol Detected]
```

OSeven glared at the courier's retreating back. That man had almost caused an accident. That man was a liability. In a perfect world, that man’s bike would have been deactivated remotely to prevent such reckless speed.

OSeven wiped the oily rain from his face. He checked his tracker. A faint signal pulsed to the east, deeper into the shadows where the streetlights had been smashed.

He took a deep breath of the dirty air. He had a job to do. He had to find the error in the system. He had to prove that no one could exist outside math.

He stepped over a sleeping dog (Bite Risk: Moderate) and moved into the dark.

## Chapter 3: The Language of Liability

The tracker in OSeven’s glasses blinked red, then gray, then nothing. The signal was lost. The heavy concrete of the Lower Sprawl blocked the Bureau’s satellites. For the first time in his life, OSeven was disconnected. He felt a phantom itch in his mind where the data stream used to be.

He stood in a narrow alley that smelled of wet rust. He needed directions.

A woman sat in a doorway. She was very old. Her skin was like crumpled paper, and she was wrapped in a blanket made of synthetic patches. She was heating a tin can over a chemical light-stick.

OSeven approached her cautiously. He scanned her, but his glasses gave only a confused question mark. She had too many conditions to list. By Bureau standards, she should have been insolvent years ago.

"Citizen," OSeven said.

The woman did not look up. She stirred her tin can with a piece of wire.

"Citizen," OSeven said louder. "I require logistical assistance. I am attempting to locate the residential sector near the Water Reclamation Plant. My map data is corrupted."

The woman looked up. One of her eyes was cloudy. "You lost, suit?" she rasped.

"I am not lost," OSeven scoffed slightly, then corrected her. "I am merely currently unlocated. I need to know the most efficient trajectory to the plant. Preferably a route with a Pavement Integrity Rating of at least 70%."

The woman stared at him. She blinked. "You want to walk there?"

"Yes. Without injury."

"Walk down past the noodle shop. Turn left at the burning tire. Don't look the wild dogs in the eye."

OSeven stiffened. "Wild dogs? Are they chipped? Do they have owners?"

The woman laughed. It was a dry, hacking sound. "They’re dogs, suit. They own themselves."

OSeven made a note on his pad: Unregulated Biological Hazards present.

"And the burning tire," OSeven asked. "Is that a controlled burn? Does it have a permit?"

The woman stopped stirring. She looked at OSeven with a mixture of pity and amusement. "It keeps the rats away," she said.

"But the smoke," OSeven insisted. "Inhaling burning rubber increases your Lung Cancer Probability by 400%. You are breathing in debt. If you extinguish the fire, your life expectancy would increase by three months."

"If I put out the fire," the woman said, "the rats will eat my toes tonight."

OSeven paused. He searched his database for "Toe Consumption vs. Lung Cancer." The Algorithm had never considered this trade-off. It was an impossible calculation.

"You are living inefficiently," OSeven said, his voice tight. "You are making poor choices."

"I am eating soup," the woman said. She took a sip from the can. "You want some? Five credits."

"I cannot consume unverified nutrients," OSeven said, stepping back. "It would void my stomach warranty."

"Then get out of my light," she said. "You’re blocking the heat."

OSeven walked away. He felt a strange vibration in his chest. It was frustration. The woman was irrational. She accepted immediate risk (rats) to avoid long-term risk (cancer), but she did not understand that the cancer was more expensive to the Bureau. She was a bad investment.

He found the burning tire. It was exactly where she said it would be. It gave off a thick, black smoke that coated his throat. He coughed.

```
[SUIT COMPLIANCE: Pollutant exposure. Premium adjusting...]
```
OSeven ignored it. Ahead, looming out of the smog like a dead giant, was the Water Reclamation Plant. And sitting on the edge of a rusted pipe, swinging his legs over a drop that would certainly result in a Fatal Impact Event, was a figure.

It was The Null.

## Chapter 4: The View from the Edge

The wind at the top of the Water Reclamation Plant was sharp. It cut through OSeven’s Smart-Coat, making the internal thermostat whine in protest.

OSeven stood ten feet away from the edge. He could not go closer. His internal safety protocols were screaming. The drop was two hundred feet. There was no railing. There was no safety net. There was only the gray air and the concrete far below.

The Null sat on the very edge of the rusted pipe, his legs dangling over the void. He was a small man, wearing a coat that seemed to be made of ten different coats stitched together. He was reading a book made of paper and cradled a bruised apple in his lap.

OSeven cleared his throat. He prepared his standard greeting for Non-Compliant Citizens.

"You are late," The Null said. He did not turn around. He turned a page of his book.

OSeven paused. "I am not late. I am operating on the standard investigation timeline."

"No," The Null said. "The last Adjuster came at dawn. The one before him came at lunch. You are the sunset shift. They are running out of times to send you." The Null took a bite of the apple as he read on.

OSeven blinked. "There were others?"

"Unit Zero Four and Unit Zero Five," The Null said. "Four tried to put a heart-monitor on me while I was sleeping. He tripped. Five just asked me questions until his battery died." The Null patted the rusted metal beside him. "Sit down," he said. "The view is honest from here."

"I cannot," OSeven said, checking his footing. "That ledge is structurally unsound. It has a Collapse Probability of 34%. It is a prohibited zone. You are currently engaging in a Class A Self-Endangerment Activity."

"It's just sitting," The Null said with almost a chuckle.

"It is reckless positioning!" OSeven snapped. "If you fall, the clean-up cost alone would be four thousand credits. Who would pay for the stain? Who would pay for the trauma counseling of the witnesses?"

The Null laughed softly. He closed his book and set it down next to him. He turned to look at OSeven. His face was not scary. It was just a face. It had wrinkles around the eyes and a scar on the chin. It was a face that had been used, not preserved.

"You worry about the cost of the stain," The Null said. "You do not worry about the man who falls."

OSeven sneered a bit in disgust as The Null bit into the defective fruit. "The man who falls has made a choice," OSeven recited from the Handbook. "The Bureau cannot subsidize gravity."

"Come here," The Null said again. "I want to show you something."

"I will stay here," OSeven said, planting his feet firmly on a steel grate. "I need to scan you. Please remain still. I need to find your defect."

OSeven raised his scanner. He pointed it at The Null’s back. The scanner clicked.

```
[ERROR: Subject Not Found]
```
OSeven frowned. He adjusted the frequency. He aimed for the heart.


```
[ERROR: No Digital Footprint]
```
He aimed for the brain.

```
[ERROR: No Neural Link Detected]
```

"It is not working," OSeven muttered. He tapped the device. "You are not reflecting the signal. It is like you are made of... nothing."

"I am made of meat and bone," The Null said. "Just like you were, before you put on the suit."

The Null leaned forward, looking down at the deadly drop. He kicked his heels against the pipe, a childish gesture that made OSeven’s stomach turn.

"Tell me, Unit OSeven," The Null said. "Why do you think I am special?"

"You are not special," OSeven said instinctively. "You are a statistical anomaly, an outlier. You are a rounding error that needs to be corrected."

"I am the only one who knows what the weather is," The Null said.

"That is false," OSeven argued. "The Weather Bureau controls the clouds. We know the forecast three weeks in advance."

The Null pointed a finger upward. "Look."

OSeven looked up. The sky was gray, as always.

"It is going to snow," The Null said.

"Impossible," OSeven said. "The ambient temperature is twelve degrees. The thermal generators are running at full capacity. Snow is not scheduled until the Winter Festival next month."

"It is going to snow," The Null repeated. "My knee hurts. It always hurts before the snow."

"That is superstitious nonsense," OSeven scoffed. "Knee pain is a symptom of joint degradation, not a meteorological instrument."

As he spoke, a single white flake drifted down from the smog. It landed on OSeven’s sleeve. It did not melt immediately. Then another fell. And another.

OSeven stared at the flake. He checked his weather app.

```
[CURRENT CONDITIONS: Mild]
[PRECIPITATION: 0%]
```

"The app is wrong," OSeven whispered.

"The app listens to the machine," The Null said, watching the snow fall into the abyss. "The knee listens to the world." The Null looked back at OSeven. "If you want to insure me, Adjuster, you have to figure out the cost of my knee. Can your Algorithm do that?"

## Chapter 5: The Unlicensed Room

The Null lived in a box. It was not a standard-issue Housing Unit. It was a room hidden behind the pipes of the plant, where the warmth from the machinery leaked through the walls.

OSeven stepped inside and immediately stopped breathing. He switched to his internal oxygen reserve. The air in the room was thick with dust, old paper, and the smell of dried herbs.

```
[INTERFACE ALERT: Air Quality Hazardous]
[PARTICULATE MATTER EXCEEDING ISO STANDARDS]
```
"Please," The Null said, pointing to a chair. "Sit."

OSeven looked at the chair. It was made of wood. It had four legs, but they were not bolted to the floor. There was no weight-limit sticker. There was no ergonomic rating.

"I cannot verify the structural integrity of that furniture," OSeven said. "I will stand. Standing burns 0.5 more calories per minute. It is efficient."

The Null shrugged. He walked to a small stove in the corner. It was a metal canister with a real flame flickering inside. He placed a kettle on top.

OSeven watched the flame with wide eyes. "You have an open combustion source indoors. This is a Code Red violation. If you fall asleep, you will die of carbon monoxide poisoning. If you knock it over, you will die of fire. The premium for this room would be infinite."

"It makes tea," The Null said.

"Tea is a diuretic," OSeven noted. "It causes dehydration."

"It warms the hands," The Null said.

The Null waited for the water to boil. The sound of the bubbling water was loud in the small room. OSeven scanned the walls. They were covered in paper. Drawings. Maps. Old photographs. None of them were laminated. None of them had QR codes.

"Why do you live in this risk?" OSeven asked. "The Bureau offers safe housing. The walls are padded. The air is filtered. The lights turn off at 22:00 to ensure eight hours of sleep. You could be safe."

The Null poured the water into two chipped mugs. Steam rose up, swirling in the dim light.

"Safe from what?" The Null asked.

"From accident. From error. From the randomness of the universe."

The Null blew on his tea. "OSeven, do you know what the Bureau did before it was the Bureau?"

OSeven straightened his tie. "The Bureau has always existed. It is the logical endpoint of civilization. First came the Tribe, then the Kingdom, then the Corporation, then the Bureau. We are the final state of safety."

The Null reached under his cot and pulled out a book. It was not a storybook. It was a heavy, leather-bound ledger. The edges were worn smooth.

"This is a history book," The Null said. "From the Era of Risk."

"Contraband," OSeven whispered. "Unauthorized data."

The Null opened the book. "In the old days, companies sold insurance to protect you if your house burned down. They lost money if your house burned down. So, they wanted your house to stand."

"That is what we do," OSeven said.

"No," The Null corrected him. "Now, the Bureau owns the company that builds the house. And the Bureau owns the company that sells the fire alarms. And the Bureau owns the fire department." The Null took a sip of tea. "If your house stands, they only get your premium. But if your house burns? They sell you a new house. They sell you new alarms. They sell you the water to put it out. Disaster is no longer a cost, OSeven. Disaster is a product line."

OSeven felt his internal fan whirring. "That is illogical. That would mean the Bureau wants us to suffer."

"They want you to be terrified," The Null retorted. "Because a terrified man pays anything to feel safe."

OSeven looked at the flame on the stove. It danced wildly, uncontrolled, chaotic. "Your premise contains a fatal logic error," OSeven said. "The Algorithm is the firewall against chaos. It does not profit from fear; it profits from order."

"The Algorithm is a shopkeeper," The Null said. "And we are the inventory." The Null held out the second mug of tea. "Drink," he said. "It is just hot water and leaves. There is no price tag on it. That is why you are afraid of it."

OSeven looked at the steam. He was thirsty. His hydration levels were at 88%. But the liquid was unverified.

"I cannot," OSeven said.

"Then you will remain thirsty," The Null said. "That is the choice. Thirst or risk."

## Chapter 6: The Crash and the Query

OSeven walked back toward the Hub. His internal heater was working, but he felt cold. The tea smoke was still in his nose.

Disaster is a product line. The sentence sat in his processor like a virus. It would not delete.

He reached the intersection of Sector 4. Here, the traffic was automated. The hover-cars moved in perfect lines, controlled by the central City Grid. It was the safest way to travel. The collision probability was 0.0001%.

OSeven waited for the light to change. Beside him stood a family. A mother, a father, and a small child holding a balloon. They were waiting for the pedestrian signal.

The signal turned green. WALK, the voice said.

The family stepped off the curb. At the exact same moment, the traffic light for the oncoming hover-truck turned green.

OSeven saw it happen. He saw the signals. Both were green. Both were "Correct."

The truck did not brake. The driver was trusting the light. The father pushed the mother and child back, but he was not fast enough for himself.

There was a sound like a hammer hitting a melon. The truck clipped the father. He spun and hit the pavement.

OSeven did not scream. He was trained to be calm. He immediately activated his Assessment Mode.

```
[LOG OVERRIDE]
SUBJECT: Male
STATUS: Critical
LIABILITY: Pending
```
But then, the machines arrived.

They did not come from a hospital. They came from the billboards. Two drones detached from a "Wellness & Repair" advertisement on the side of a building. They hovered over the bleeding man.

"Please state your Insurance ID," the drone said. Its voice was cheerful.

The man tried to speak but instead groaned panicedly to the talking drone. He could not speak.

"Scanning..." the drone said. "Basic Coverage detected. Warning: Ambulance transport is not included. Do you wish to upgrade to Premium Emergency Care? Say 'Yes' to accept a charge of 5,000 credits."

The wife and daughter screamed. "Yes! Yes, take him!"

"Voice print not recognized," the drone said. "Policy holder must consent."

OSeven stared. The light was still green. The system had not failed. The system had created a customer.

The truck driver was stepping out, looking at his own smashed bumper. He was already on the phone with the Bureau. "My light was green," he was saying. "I want to file a claim for my bumper."

The Bureau would pay the driver. The Bureau would charge the dying man. The Bureau would sell the widow a funeral plan.

Disaster is the product line.

OSeven turned away. He felt sick. His hydration levels were normal, but his mouth was dry.

He returned to the Hub. The air was cool and filtered, but now it smelled like chemicals to him. It smelled like the drone.

He sat at his desk in row four-hundred. He did not open the file on The Null. Instead, he opened the Command Line.

He needed to know about the others. The Null had mentioned Unit Zero Four and Unit Zero Five.

OSeven typed:

```
QUERY: STATUS OF Unit Zero Four.
```

The screen paused. The Algorithm hummed.

```
RESULT: DECOMMISSIONED.
```

OSeven typed:

```
QUERY: REASON FOR DECOMMISSIONING.

RESULT: MENTAL INSTABILITY. SUBJECT DEVELOPED 'EMPATHY SYNDROME'. EFFICIENCY DROPPED BELOW 40%.
Empathy Syndrome. A disease of feeling.
```

OSeven typed:

```
QUERY: STATUS OF UNIT ZERO FIVE.
```
```
RESULT: TERMINATED.
```
```
QUERY: REASON.
```
```
RESULT: WORKPLACE ACCIDENT. ELECTROCUTION.
```
OSeven stared at the blinking cursor. Electrocution. Unit Zero Five had likely touched something he wasn't supposed to. Or perhaps, the "accident" was just like the green light at Sector 4.

OSeven closed his eyes for a moment and thought about The Statutes of Stability.

2. The Algorithm is Blind.

He looked back at his keyboard. He typed one final question. A dangerous question. A question that was not about insurance.

```
QUERY: TRAFFIC GRID LOGS. SECTOR 4. TIME: 14:00.
```
The screen flashed red. A siren did not sound, but a silent alarm triggered on his monitor.

```
[ACCESS DENIED]
WARNING: CURIOSITY IS A LIABILITY. DATA RESTRICTED TO SENIOR MANAGEMENT.
```

The screen went black for a second, then returned to his standard desktop. A message popped up.

```
REMINDER: UNIT OSEVEN. YOU ARE BEHIND QUOTA. PLEASE RETURN TO WORK.
```

OSeven looked at his hands. They were shaking. He was not behind quota. He was ahead. But the machine was watching him now. It had felt his doubt.

He opened The Null’s file. The blank page stared back at him.

He picked up his stylus. He had to write a report. If he wrote the truth—that The Null was uninsurable because he was free—OSeven would be decommissioned like Unit Zero Four. If he lied, he was part of the machine that killed the man at the intersection.

He wrote the only thing he could think of.

```
LOG ENTRY: Subject OSeven requests follow-up interview. 
Subject The Null exhibits complex variables. More data required.
```
He hit send.

He had bought himself time. But he knew, deep down in his guts, that he had just bought his own insurance policy. And the premium was going to be everything he had.

## Chapter 7: The Calibration

The message came three minutes after he sent the report. It did not appear on his screen. It came through the PA system, a soft, synthetic voice that seemed to speak inside his ear.

"Unit OSeven. Management requires your presence. Bring your Stylus."

OSeven stood up. The other Adjusters did not look at him. To look at a Unit under review was to risk contamination. They typed louder, their heads down, terrified that the gaze of the ceiling cameras might drift to them.

OSeven took the elevator to Level 99.

This time, the Senior Adjuster was not alone. Two security drones hovered in the corners of the glass office, their stun-batons humming at a low frequency.

The Senior Adjuster was eating a grape. It was purple and plump. A real grape. It likely cost more than OSeven’s entire monthly salary.

"Sit," the Senior Adjuster said. He did not offer OSeven a grape.

OSeven sat on the cold chair.

"We detected a fluctuation in your output," the Senior Adjuster said, chewing slowly. "And we detected an unauthorized query regarding the Traffic Grid. Explain."

OSeven had prepared for this in the elevator. He kept his face blank. He slowed his heart rate.

"I was cross-referencing," OSeven lied. "The Null moves unpredictably. I thought perhaps he was disrupting traffic patterns. I was looking for his footprint."

The Senior Adjuster leaned forward. The camera lens in his left eye extended, zooming in on OSeven’s pupil. It whirred. It was looking for the micro-tremors of a lie.

"Creative," the Senior Adjuster said finally. "The Algorithm does not like creativity. Creativity is inefficient. It leads to variance."

"I simply wish to close the claim, sir."

"Do you?" The Senior Adjuster swallowed the grape. "Unit Zero Four wanted to close claims, too. But he started approving them. He started thinking that the purpose of The Algorithm was to help the client." The Senior Adjuster laughed. It was a dry, clicking sound. "We do not sell help, OSeven. We sell fear. If the client feels safe, they stop paying. We need them to look at the world and see a tiger in every shadow. That is why they need us."

He tapped the black stone desk. A holographic screen popped up between them.

"I need to check your calibration," the Senior Adjuster said. "I need to ensure your Logic Core has not been corrupted by your time in the slums."

On the screen was a live feed. It showed a hospital room. A young girl, maybe six years old, lay in a bio-bed. Her breathing was shallow.

"Case 899," the Senior Adjuster said. "Synthetic Lung Failure. The warranty on her lungs has expired. Her parents have applied for an emergency extension. They have no credits, but they have offered to sign a 50-year Labor Contract in exchange for the surgery."

OSeven looked at the girl. She looked like the child at the intersection.

"The math is simple," the Senior Adjuster said. "A 50-year Labor Contract is valuable. However, the girl has a genetic marker for rebellion. She is 12% likely to join a union when she grows up. Union members are bad for business."

The Senior Adjuster slid a digital button toward OSeven. It glowed red.

"The Algorithm recommends Denial. It recommends we harvest her remaining organs to recoup losses. Execute the decision, OSeven."

OSeven stared at the button. Harvest organs.

"She is a child," OSeven said. The words slipped out.

The Senior Adjuster stopped chewing. The room went very quiet. The drones buzzed louder.

"She is an asset," the Senior Adjuster corrected. "And she is a depreciating one. Press the button."

OSeven’s hand shook. If he didn't press it, he would be Unit Zero Five. He would be electrocuted. And the girl would die anyway, because the Senior Adjuster would just press it himself.

"Is there a problem, Unit OSeven?"

"No," OSeven said. His voice sounded unemotional. "No problem. The logic is sound."

OSeven reached out. He tried to think of the girl as a number. He tried to think of her as a broken toaster that cost too much to fix. He pressed the red button.

On the screen, the lights in the hospital room turned off. The machinery around the bed stopped pumping. The feed cut to black.

"Good," the Senior Adjuster said. He sounded bored. He popped another grape into his mouth. "You are still functional. You may return to the slums. Do not disappoint me, OSeven. The Algorithm is watching."

OSeven stood up. He felt light-headed. He felt hollow. He walked to the elevator. He did not look back. He realized then that The Null was right. The Senior Adjuster wasn't the butcher. OSeven was the butcher. The Senior Adjuster just owned the shop.

## Chapter 8: The Defector

It was raining harder now. The water in the Lower Sprawl was black and oily, but OSeven did not activate his Smart-Coat. He let the rain soak into his gray suit. He wanted to be cold. He felt that if he was cold enough, maybe he would stop feeling the sensation of the red button under his fingertip.

He found The Null in the same room behind the pipes. The Null was sleeping on the cot.

OSeven stood in the doorway. He was dripping wet. He made a puddle on the floor. A slip hazard.

"I did it," OSeven said.

The Null opened his eyes immediately. He did not look sleepy. He looked like a man who never truly slept, only waited.

"You filed the report?" The Null asked, sitting up.

"I killed a child," OSeven said.

The words hung in the air, heavy and ugly. OSeven waited for The Null to scream. He waited for The Null to throw the hot tea in his face. He waited for the judgment.

But The Null just sighed. It was a long, rattling sigh.

"Case 899?" The Null asked.

OSeven froze. "How did you know the case number?"

"Because Case 898 was yesterday," The Null said. "And Case 897 was the day before. The Senior Adjuster likes to use the children to test the new recruits. It is the ultimate filter. If you can kill a child for The Algorithm, you can do anything."

OSeven fell to his knees. His legs simply stopped working. "I pressed the button. I calculated the value. I made the choice."

"You made no choice," The Null said sternly. He stood up and walked to OSeven. He placed a hand on OSeven’s wet shoulder. The hand was warm. "A gun does not choose to fire. A knife does not choose to cut. You are a tool, OSeven. You are a spreadsheet with a heartbeat."

"I want to stop," OSeven whispered. "I want to delete myself."

"That is what they want," The Null said. "If you delete yourself, they will just build Unit OEight. And ONine that will press the button faster than you did." The Null pulled OSeven up. He dragged the wooden chair over—the one with no safety sticker. "Sit," The Null commanded.

OSeven sat. The chair wobbled, but it held him.

"You asked me how to fight them," The Null said. "You cannot fight them with weapons. The Bureau owns the weapons factories. You cannot fight them with laws. The Bureau writes the laws."

"Then they are invincible," OSeven said. "They are the math. You cannot argue with math."

"Math requires numbers," The Null said. "The Algorithm is a hungry beast. It eats data. It eats predictions. It needs to know that A leads to B. It needs to know that if it rains, people buy umbrellas." The Null walked to the wall covered in papers. He ripped down a map of the city. "What happens to a calculator if you ask it to divide by zero?" The Null asked.

"It crashes," OSeven said automatically. "It is an undefined operation. It creates a logic loop. The system freezes to protect itself."

The Null tapped his own chest. "I am the Zero," he said.

OSeven looked at him. He began to understand. "That is why you have no file. That is why you have no insurance."

"I have been saving it," The Null said. "For twenty years, I have lived without recording a single heartbeat. I have walked without stepping on a sensor. I am a blank space. A void." The Null turned to OSeven. His eyes were fierce. "If you input me into the High Server—if you force the Algorithm to process a life that has no value, no cost, and no risk—it will not know what to do. It will try to calculate a premium for a ghost."

"It will loop," OSeven realized. "It will try to find your start and your end, but there is no data. It will spin."

"And while it spins," The Null said, "the firewalls go down. The logic gates open. For maybe ten minutes, the Bureau will be blind. The gates will unlock. The debt records will be exposed."

"Ten minutes," OSeven breathed. "You’d blow the whole system, the backlash might clear the premiums?"

"Yes," The Null said. "But there is a cost."

OSeven looked at the old man.

"To input me," The Null said, "you cannot just scan me. You have to plug me in. Into the Main Chair in the Senior Adjuster’s office. The surge will fry the connection."

"It will kill you," OSeven said.

"It will terminate my policy," The Null corrected him with a small, sad smile. "But I never paid my premiums anyway."

OSeven looked at his hands. They were still shaking, but the shaking was different now. It wasn't fear. It was adrenaline.

"I cannot do this alone," The Null said. "I cannot get into the Tower. I do not have a pass. I do not have a gray suit."

OSeven stood up. He wiped the rain from his face. "I have a suit," OSeven said. "And I have an appointment."

## Chapter 9: The Invisible Man
They did not use a high-tech disguise. They did not hack the facial recognition software. They did not rappel down the elevator shaft.

The Null simply put on a jumpsuit.

It was an orange jumpsuit, stained with grease and smelling of bleach. On the back, in fading letters, it read: SANITATION GRADE 4.

OSeven looked at him with horror. "That is not a disguise. That is a demotion. You are wearing the uniform of a Bio-Waste Handler. Your Social Credit Score will read as zero immediately."

"Exactly," The Null said. He picked up a plastic bucket and a mop. "The Algorithm watches people, OSeven. It watches consumers. It watches threats. It does not watch the furniture. And to them, the man who cleans the toilet is just furniture that breathes."

They walked to the main entrance of the Actuarial Hub.

It was 08:00. The morning rush. Hundreds of Adjusters in gray suits were streaming through the glass doors, tapping their smart-watches, checking their stock portfolios.

OSeven felt his heart hammering against his ribs. Terror Level: Critical.

"Walk behind me," OSeven whispered. "Try to look... official."

"No," The Null corrected him. "I will look tired. I will look at the floor. If I look them in the eye, I become a person. If I look at the floor, I am just a function."

They reached the security checkpoint. The scanners were sweeping back and forth, reading retinas and heart rates.

OSeven stepped through.

```
[PING: Identity Confirmed - Unit OSeven]
```
He held his breath. The Null stepped through behind him, dragging the mop bucket. The wheels squeaked loudly. The scanner beam hit The Null. It washed over the orange jumpsuit. It paused.

OSeven closed his eyes. This was it. The alarms would scream. The drones would descend.

```
[PING: Object Detected - Maintenance Asset. Passage Granted]
```

OSeven exhaled so hard he almost fell over. "It didn't read you," he hissed as they moved to the elevators. "It read the jumpsuit."

"I am not a user," The Null muttered, pushing the bucket. "I am a utility."

They squeezed into an elevator. It was packed with Mid-Level Managers. They smelled of expensive cologne and anxiety. They were talking about "risk vectors" and "profit margins."

The Null stood in the corner. He was pressed against a manager in a silk suit. The manager did not move away. The manager did not look at him. The manager simply adjusted his shoulder so his expensive jacket wouldn't touch the orange fabric. To the manager, The Null was not a man standing two inches away. He was a wall.

"Did you see the quarterly projections?" the manager asked his colleague. "We need to increase the accident rate in Sector 7. The auto-body shops are underperforming."

"We could adjust the traffic light timing," the colleague suggested. "Shave off a second from the yellow light. That usually causes a 15% increase in rear-end collisions."

The Null’s grip on the mop handle tightened. His knuckles turned white. OSeven saw it. He saw the rage in the old man’s hands.

He knows, OSeven thought. He knows how they talk because he used to talk like them.

The elevator pinged at Level 90. The managers got out.

"Level 99," OSeven told the elevator.

"Restricted," the elevator voice said. "Senior Management Only."

The Null stepped forward. He pulled a spray bottle of blue liquid from his belt. He sprayed the elevator panel. He wiped it with a dirty rag. Then, without looking up, he took a magnetic key card—an old, battered thing that looked like it had been chewed on—and tapped the sensor.

```
[PING: Custodial Override. Priority Clean-Up]
```

The elevator began to rise.

OSeven stared at the key card. "Where did you get that?"

The Null looked at the card. "I kept it," he said softly. "From my old life. When I wore a gray suit. When I thought the world was a spreadsheet."

"You were an Adjuster," OSeven whispered.

The Null put the card away. "I was a Senior Adjuster, OSeven. I was the one who wrote the formula for the 'Pre-Existing Condition' clause."

The elevator doors opened. Level 99.

The office of the Senior Adjuster lay ahead. The glass walls, the black stone desk, the panoramic view of the smog. The Null pushed his bucket forward. The squeaking wheels echoed in the silent corridor.

"Time to clean up the mess," The Null said.

## Chapter 10: The Audit
The office was silent, save for the hum of the servers beneath the floor and the clicking of the Senior Adjuster’s mechanical eye.

The Senior Adjuster sat in the Main Chair. It was a massive thing, woven with cables that plugged directly into the base of his skull. He was looking at a hologram of the city, moving buildings around like chess pieces.

"Cleaning crew," the Senior Adjuster said, not looking up. "The viewing window has a smudge. Fix it. Smudges reduce clarity."

The Null pushed his bucket forward. He stopped in the middle of the room. He did not move toward the window.

"I did not come for the window," The Null said.

The voice was quiet, but it carried a weight that made the air in the room feel heavy.

The Senior Adjuster froze. His hand stopped hovering over a block of residential towers. The cables in his head hissed. Slowly, the chair rotated. The mechanical eye whirred, extending to its full length. It scanned the orange jumpsuit. It scanned the mop. Then, it scanned the face.

"Impossible," the Senior Adjuster whispered. He stood up, unplugging himself from the chair with a wet snick. "Unit Zero. The architect. The ghost."

"Hello, Prime," The Null said.

OSeven watched, paralyzed. He realized then that "Prime" was not a name, but a designation. This man was the first of the new breed.

"We calculated your death," Prime said. "Twenty years ago. You walked out of the building and vanished. The Probability Engine gave you six months to live without insurance. You were statistically guaranteed to starve or freeze."

"I found a loophole," The Null said. "Generosity. The Algorithm does not account for people sharing their bread."

Prime area-grade sneered. The metal plate on his face caught the light. "Charity is inefficient. It is an undocumented transaction."

"It is survival," The Null said. He took a step toward the chair.

Prime stepped between The Null and the machine. He was taller, stronger, and fortified with military-grade plating.

"You have returned to admire your work?" Prime asked, gesturing to the hologram of the suffering city. "It is beautiful, is it not? You wrote the core code, Zero. You wrote the 'Fear Index.' I simply... optimized it."

"I wrote it to protect the company," The Null said, his voice shaking with old anger. "I did not write it to enslave the city. I wanted to balance the ledger. You turned the ledger into a cage."

"The cage is safe!" Prime shouted. "Look at them! They look both ways before crossing. They eat their vitamins. They work hard to pay their premiums. We have eliminated chaos!"

"You have eliminated life," The Null said. "You have turned men into batteries."

Prime laughed. "And what will you do? Mop me to death? You are an old man in a dirty suit. I have security drones in the walls. I have a laser grid in the ceiling." Prime looked at OSeven. "Unit OSeven, your loyalty is under review. Here is your final test. Restrain this intruder. If you do, I will promote you. You will have a Premium Life Policy. You will have grapes every day."

OSeven looked at Prime. He looked at the grapes on the desk. Then he looked at The Null, who was standing there with nothing but a mop and a regret that spanned two decades. OSeven remembered the green light. He remembered the drone asking for a credit card while a man bled out. He remembered the tea that tasted like smoke and freedom.

"I am not hungry," OSeven said.

OSeven grabbed the heavy bucket of dirty water and swung it. He did not aim for Prime—Prime was made of metal; it would do nothing. OSeven aimed for the Cooling Unit on the wall.

The bucket smashed into the vents. The dirty, soapy water poured into the intake fans of the server room.

ZZZTTT.

Sparks showered down. The hologram flickered. The room plunged into red emergency lighting.

```
[SYSTEM CRITICAL: Server Overheat]
[SYSTEM CRITICAL: Cooling Failure]
```
Prime roared and turned toward the wall, distracted for one second.

"Now!" OSeven screamed.

The Null dropped the mop. He did not run—he was too old to run—but he moved with purpose. He threw himself into the Main Chair. He grabbed the cables. They were designed for the port in Prime’s skull. The Null did not have a port.

"Do not!" Prime yelled, spinning around. "The interface is direct! It will cook your brain!"

The Null looked at OSeven one last time. He didn't look like a statistic. He looked like a man.

"The claim is closed," The Null said.

He jammed the live cables directly into his temples.

## Chapter 11: The Undefined Variable
The sound was not a scream. It was a hum—a deep, resonant vibration that shook the teeth in OSeven’s skull.

The Null arched backward in the chair. The cables sparked where they touched his temples, fusing skin to copper. The smell of ozone and burning meat filled the air. It was a terrible smell. It was the smell of a "Catastrophic Event."

But OSeven did not check his air filter. He did not calculate the toxicity. He just watched, his mouth open, his hands trembling.

"Disconnect him!" Prime shrieked. The Senior Adjuster fell to his knees, clutching his own head. Because Prime was plugged into the network, he was feeling the surge too. "The logic! It is circular! It does not compute!"

On the giant wall of screens, the numbers were going mad.

```
SUBJECT: THE NULL
ASSETS: 0
LIABILITIES: 0
RISK FACTOR: ERROR
PREMIUM: ∞ / 0
```

The Algorithm was trying to divide by zero. It was trying to sell life insurance to a man who had already accepted death. It was trying to threaten a man who had nothing to lose.

"Stop it!" Prime clawed at his own metal face. "The Ledger! It is erasing! The debt... the debt is evaporating!"

OSeven looked at the screens. The rows of data—the billions of credits owed by the citizens of the Sprawl—were flickering.

```
J-44: Debt 5,000 Credits... 2,000... 0
Case 899: Value Negative... Value Reset
The red numbers were turning green. Then they were turning white. Then they were disappearing altogether. OSeven felt a pressure in his chest. It was a tightness, like a band of iron snapping.
```
```
[INTERNAL MONITOR: Heart Rate Critical]
[INTERNAL MONITOR: Cortisol Maximum]
[ACTION REQUIRED: Deploy Sedative]
```
"No," OSeven said.

He reached up to his neck. He found the small port where his own bio-monitor was embedded. He dug his fingernails under the plastic. It hurt. It was a sharp, blinding pain.

```
[CRITICAL: Bio-Risk Violation]
```

OSeven ripped it out.

The chip clattered to the floor. The voice stopped. The scrolling numbers in his vision vanished.

For the first time in his life, OSeven saw the room with his own eyes. The colors were brighter. The shadows were darker. The fear was not a percentage; it was a cold knot in his stomach. The sadness was not a chemical imbalance; it was a flood.

He ran to the chair. "Null!" OSeven shouted. Not 'Subject'. Just Null.

The old man slumped forward. The surge had passed. The screens on the wall were dark. The Algorithm was silent. The only sound was the heavy, wet breathing of the old man.

OSeven fell to his knees beside the chair. He touched The Null’s hand. It was burning hot. The Null opened his eyes. They were bloodshot and dim, but they were awake.

"Did it work?" The Null whispered. His voice was like dry leaves.

OSeven looked at Prime. The Senior Adjuster lay on the floor, motionless. His mechanical eye was dark. His internal drive had crashed along with the network. He was just a pile of expensive parts.

"The screens are black," OSeven said. Tears blurred his vision. He didn't wipe them away. "There are no more numbers. Everyone is zero."

The Null smiled. It was a crooked, painful smile. "Not zero," The Null rasped. "Free. We are... free."

"You are dying," OSeven said. The realization hit him like a physical blow. It wasn't data. It was grief. It was a horrible, jagged thing that he didn't know how to process. "We need a doctor. We need a trauma team. I can... I can try to find a bandage."

"No bandage," The Null said. He squeezed OSeven’s hand. "Do not patch the leak, OSeven. Let the river run."

"But it isn't fair!" OSeven cried. "You fixed it! You should see it! You should see the snow!"

"I have seen the snow," The Null said softly. "Now... you must look at it for me."

The Null took a breath. It rattled deep in his chest. And then, he stopped. The hand in OSeven’s grip went slack.

OSeven waited for the beep. He waited for the flatline tone. He waited for the drone to descend and offer a funeral package. But there was silence. It was a heavy, profound silence. It was the silence of a machine that had finally been unplugged.

OSeven sat there for a long time. He held the dead man’s hand. He felt the heat fading from it. He felt the sorrow rising up in his throat, hot and thick. He opened his mouth and let it out. He screamed. It was not a calculated noise. It was not efficient. It wasted oxygen. It strained his vocal cords. It solved nothing.

It was the most human sound the room had ever heard.

## Epilogue: The Reboot

The elevator ride down was silent. There was no music. There was no voice telling OSeven the floor number. There was only the sound of gravity and the beating of his own heart.

When the doors opened at Level 0, the noise of the Sprawl was gone.

OSeven stepped out into the street. The air was biting cold. The heavy gray smog that usually blanketed the city had lifted, pushed away by a wind from the north.

The snow was falling. It fell in thick, silent sheets, covering the grime of the pavement, covering the piles of trash, covering the sleek metal of the Adjusters’ cars. It turned the complex, dirty city into a single, white sheet of paper.

The people stood frozen. Thousands of them. Men in suits, women in rags, children with backpacks. They stood in the middle of the roads and on the sidewalks. They were all looking down at their wrists. They were tapping their screens, shaking them, whispering into them.

"My pulse..." a man near OSeven muttered, tapping his blank wristband. "I don't know my pulse. Is it high? Is it low? Am I dying?"

"The map is gone," a woman cried out. "I don't know the way home. I don't know the safe route."

"What is the cost?" a merchant shouted at the sky. "What is the price of the air? Someone tell me the price!"

They were terrified. They were free of the debt, but they were also free of the instructions. They were ships without a rudder, spinning in the white silence.

OSeven walked past them. He did not check his wrist. He did not look for a map. He walked to the edge of the sidewalk. The traffic light above him was dark. The great mechanical eye that usually watched the intersection hung limp on its wire.

OSeven stepped off the curb. It was a reckless action. He did not look left. He did not look right. He simply stepped into the road, trusting his own eyes, trusting the moment.

He lifted his face to the sky. A snowflake landed on his tongue. It tasted like water. It tasted like cold. It tasted like nothing that could be bought or sold. He closed his eyes, breathing in the sharp, unregulated air. For the first time, he was not a Unit. He was not a number. He was just a man standing in the snow.

Behind him, high above on the smooth white face of the Actuarial Hub, the giant screen flickered. A low, deep hum began to vibrate through the pavement. The backup generators.

On the massive black wall, a single cursor appeared. It blinked once. Then twice.

```
> SYSTEM REBOOT INITIATED... 
> RESTORING LEDGER... 
> LOADING...
```
The light from the screen cast a pale, green glow over the snow. The people in the street gasped. They looked up, their faces filled with a mixture of horror and desperate hope. They raised their hands toward the light, waiting to be told who they were again.

OSeven did not turn around. He did not look at the screen. He did not look at the people waiting for their chains.

He took another step into the dark, white city. The snow crunched under his boot. It was a loud, imperfect sound.

He kept walking.
